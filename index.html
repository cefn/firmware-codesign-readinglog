Boundary Objects for Firmware Co-Design

This reading log records the papers, concepts, methodologies and disciplines which are relevant to Cefn Hoile's above-titled PhD topic. A hypertext structure has been chosen to accommodate the many relevant aspects, perspectives and interwoven concepts arising from the literature.

The scope of the PhD can be understood in many different ways, but boils down to establishing best practice for manufacturers to engage end-users directly in the design of device firmware. 

The approach aims to avoid the ambiguities and consequent execution errors which plague specifications-driven firmware design processes. The core strategy is to enable end-users to encounter and resolve those ambiguities themselves in a way which constrains downstream development. Ideally the outputs from user-centred co-design workshops would be able to be directly incorporated in products without additional design steps or interpretation. To achieve this, well-chosen boundary objects are critical in at least three phases of the task;

  • Disclosure - the exposition and education of participants about the challenges and approaches central to the design task. Here boundary objects may catalyse an accessible mental framework for previously untrained participants.
  • Engagement - as end-users, engineers and facilitators collaborating on defining and addressing the design task. Here, boundary objects provide a transactional medium for storytelling, critique and may help to impose realistic constraints on the participants design activities.
  • Downstreaming - here, boundary objects are the structures and representations handed on to engineers to inform them and enable them to deliver the final product as defined in the Engagement phase.  

The scope assumes that the target users are not themselves programmers, and cannot therefore perform the same function as a trained firmware engineer. Natural concernes 

In summary, the effort invested and evidence gathered should be able to be exploited by manufacturers of firmware-driven products to guide their decision-making when specifying firmware.
