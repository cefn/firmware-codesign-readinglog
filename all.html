Total Design: Integrated Methods for Successful Product Engineering - Stuart Pugh

Notes

Explores the notion of total design - practiced in the real world, as opposed to partial design - the normal way people encounter design when they are being taught it. The book rests on the notion that without specific interventions, people's partial design training will lead to problematic design outcomes. The book presents a very commercially-oriented model of design, with a strong emphasis on 'engineering' through to sales. It has an unreconstructed view of information flow forward in the product design process, (though this may be a result of an acknowledged view of the product design in hindsight). It uses concrete examples of 'total design' such as  a lifting truck, a wind tunnel, surgical robotics. The 'waterfall' forward design approach is illustrated well by the conceptual diagrams (e.g. Figure 2.2 p 32 - outlines a set of sources proceeding to a formal 'product design specification', figure 2.9 p43 shows information flow from specification to sales, but not back again). A set of formal analysis approaches are proposed; Parametric analysis - a way in which product design spaces can be plotted parametrically to identify opportunities or outliers with respect to existing competitors. Needs analysis is about gathering evidence of customer need. Matrix analysis is a tickbox representation of competitors' products' features.

In summary 'total design' adds up to not-getting-it-wrong and doesn't seem to have another definition, apart from perhaps taking the series of proposed approaches by Pugh and simply executing all of them in sequence.

Schemata, Gambits and Precedent: Some Factors in Design Expertise

Notes

Referenced as a major conceptual foundation by Dan Lockton regards his "Design with Intent" workshopping cards.  They talk about design 'gambits' drawing a parallel with chess players, who do not exhaustively or analytically approach a game, but instead their expertise relies on 'experience' and 'recognition' of the board state. Similarly, they explore studies of conversation within an architectural practice, and descriptions of two-dimensional images shared  between graphical designers, concluding they rely on a shared base of 'precedent' references. They note the dominance of story, e.g. within the architectural practice, and characterise forms of memory in use as being 'episodic' (narrative) rather than 'semantic' (theoretical/verbal?)

This suggests a line of enquiry for me, that users may be taught the value-system (win/lose analogy with chess) and the gambits of programming through preparing their recognition of problem situations and possible solutions. This could mean they can act intelligently within the program design space, without necessarily being coders. They may recognise the structure of a failure condition without necessarily having to demonstrate it (needing a digitally programmed prototype). With sufficient priming in this space, it may be possible for domain-led program design discussion to be sustained by non-programmers.

They draw upon the concept of affordance as a proposed organising principle for precedents, so that designers can match problem cases to possible stories or previous scenarios.

They interestingly draw out this contrast of being "solution focused" (designerly?) versus "problem focused" (analytical?). Could this relate to hacking versus computer science - combining designerly strategies with code?)

Notes

Lit review, covering experimental materials supporting 'conceptual modeling' in systems analysis and design, substantiated by 'meaningful tests of domain semantics' - "research aimed at empirically evaluating ... diagrammatic notations... to convey semantics of a real world domain". They proceed to analyse prior experiments within a model of evidential quality and procedural legitimacy which they outline at the beginning of the paper.

They focus on formal validity questions, e.g. controlled variables in subject selection, choice of lab versus ex-lab. Four contrasting experimental design aspects are highlighted, independent variables, dependent measures, participants and procedures.

Recommend 'informational equivalence' so that the semantic delivery of different representations.
