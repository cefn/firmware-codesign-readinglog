Relevance

Fischer is a key writer about meta-design; "designing for design", which intersects strongly with the problem of creating assets and learning trajectories for co-design workshops. This is an incredibly resonant paper for my work, given that it connects to co-design, but also design of software, the externalisation of constraints and in particular the externalisation through computational critics (automated problem-finding processes).

Notes

This paper refers to a number of co-design workshop events supported with a "Domain-Oriented design environment" - a computational infrastructure to support reflection for different design domains. An example cited is a system for stakeholders to assign buses to routes in Boulder, Colorado (where the research team are based). These are domain-specific tools which support participants by allowing them to learn fundamentals about the problem, as well as learn perspectives from others, by externalising their thinking into a computer-supported scratch space which embodies the problem landscape. The authors assert that a single individual cannot be expected to have insight into all the important aspects of a real-world design, demanding that specific effort is invested in creating the conditions for exchange and consolidation of multiple stakeholders' insights. Designing for sharing within a design workshop helps others who have complementary knowledge (hence 'symmetry of ignorance') to engage with and collaborate over each others issues of concern. The computer supported design environment helps to make constraints from the domain explicit as part of participants' design exploration, (such as how quickly buses can get around, for example), which are implemented through simulated computational entities (having behaviours of their own, crafted to reflect the domain).

This paper reads like a position paper, which introduces key phrases, but relates them to a thread of research activity which is presented without a great deal of implementation detail. In general the authors are agitating for acknowledgement of meta-design as a recognised field of design, in which environments are crafted to help others engage in design through 'social creativity' and 'collaborative knowledge construction'. In particular they recognise the creative value of 'breakdowns' in which expectations cannot be achieved because of constraints in the domain, and rely on 'objects-to-think-with' to surface these breakdowns in a way that participants can address them.

The particular approach they recommend might apply very well to agent-oriented systems such as public-transport networks, in which each vehicle can be presented as an agent with simulated/programmed behaviour. This suggests that the approach only applies to a narrow set of domains, where this encoding of agent behaviours is feasible/comprehensible. This very important question is not explored within the paper.
