"Findings from my early studies at Xerox PARC showed that modes were very difficult for people to deal with.
People were always getting into the wrong mode and did not know how to get back out."

"even an idea that seems elegant and that satisfies some principle is not necessarily going to succeed. The only way to find out is to test it."

"To make the tests of maximum benefit to the
designers, there are certain rules we try to
follow:

  • Don't offer help too soon;
  • Welcome suggestions and reactions;
  • Observe but don't pressure"

"Even if the subject makes an obvious statement, a trivial remark, or suggests something tha% is impossible to do on a computer, it is important to welcome his reactions and discuss them as an equal."

"We start out asking open questions. "Well, what do you think?" or "What was hard; what was easy?" Later we pose more direct questions like "The
time you made that certain error, what were you thinking?" or "What do you think is a better term for this concept?" Usually in the open-question stage we get some really good responses that are unexpected. The directed questions address more specific issues."

"Occasionally, some user's unique quirk may be worth memtioning~ but what is of most concern is what most or all users experienced."
