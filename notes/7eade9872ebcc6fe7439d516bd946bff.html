An Exploratory Study of Forces and Frictions affecting Large-Scale Model-Driven Development

Authors

Adrian Kuhn, Gail C. Murphy, and C. Albert Thompson

Notes

Linked to UBC - a very interesting location in the world for paragliding :)

They look into cognitive issues of using model-driven approach in software engineering for cars (firmware) drawing on interviews and using the metaphor of force and friction, in a way which is intended to transcend the specific tools used, and apply to model-driven in general.

These forces and frictions, and the form of analysis used, may transfer to the study of (representational) boundary objects for firmware engineering.

Also interesting are the (multi-layered) representations inside Simulink, and the other representations, including formal transformation rules, and informal 'patches' which accrete around it.

Deserves a proper read-through, having just cherry-picked the important concepts this time, especially to decide whether these 'forces and frictions' transfer to firmware co-design.

"Without the linear order of text lines, which is superimposed upon source code, visual programming as found in models has no linear reading path and can possibly stretch in all directions, left, right, top, bottom, and even down to the next nesting level. While modeling guidelines try to alleviate this by imposing a flow from top-left to bottom-right, engineers struggle with reading visual models as to make sure they are not missing a part of their work to-be-done."

"While architectural complexity is well hidden from software engineers, they are still exposed to substantial innate complexity (contextual forces) and often even new accidental complexity (points of frictions in modeling tools). In particular, the representational abstraction of visual modeling languages does not seem to be as broad and rich as the problem-specific visual and formal languages of the domain experts."

"While the MDE tools under study provide specialized code-generation engineers with powerful abstraction, they do not similarly empower its end-users, i.e., domain experts and software engineers, to express their own problem-specific languages and type systems."
