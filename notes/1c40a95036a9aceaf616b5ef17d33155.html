Sketching Sketching: Outlines of a Collaborative Design Method

Authors

Brock Craft, Paul Cairns

Notes

Brock was a colleague at Tinkerlondon. Reports on 'sketching' as a technique (see also CHI paper which places sketching as central to all design). A broad literature review around sketching and collaborative design. Worth re-reading whenever covering a section on sketching as boundary object within the PhD.

Quotations

"Our research has uncovered some of the evidence that outlines why sketching is useful and why it is therefore so central to...activities in...design-oriented disciplines [such as] architecture, engineering, and visual communication"

"Sketching is regarded as an important part of design activity, primarily because it supports cognition in ways that lead to creativity"

"research has provided concrete evidence for the value and mechanisms of sketching in three ways: by empirically demonstrating the design value of sketching, articulating the cognitive support it provides, and describing how this is useful for fostering creativity"

"Sketching was found to be valuable because it supports individual re-interpretive cycles of idea generation, and enhances individual"and group access to earlier ideas." [reporting on Van der Lugt]

"Their work also showed that by providing an external representation with which team members could interact, sketching enhanced collaborative activities." [this is a worthwhile dimension (stimulating collaboration, beyond mere communication)]

"In terms of creativity and cognition, two concepts recur in the literature: lateral transformations (i.e., lateral thinking) and cycles of generation and interpretation, or “dialectic”. These two concepts are seen as cognitive benefits which sketching provides and which foster activities generative of creativity."

"Fällman [9] also describes sketching as having important cognitive effects because it parallels designers’ thinking processes. He sees sketching as ‘not primarily a tool, technique, or skill that is available to designers, but rather as the way in which designers think’. Because of this, Fällman (like Buxton), considers sketching to be archetypal of design."

"Goel [11] identifies important distinctions between ill-structured problems and well-structured problems, and argues that ‘some ill-structured problems require “ill-structured” representations to prevent premature crystallization of ideas and facilitate the generation and exploration of alternate solutions’."
