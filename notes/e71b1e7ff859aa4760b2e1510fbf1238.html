Participatory ergonomics in design processes: The role of boundary objects

Authors

Ole Broberg, Vibeke Andersen, Rikke Seim

Year

Notes

This is the summary of analysis of many Participatory Ergonomics activities over many years [wikipedia:The participatory approach to ergonomics relies on actively involving workers in implementing ergonomic knowledge, procedures and changes with the intention of improving working conditions, safety, productivity, quality, morale and/or comfort] .

They group boundary objects encountered in actual PE activities into 5 categories, then go on to identify 8 qualities which boundary objects may have. Many are listed under each category, based on Carlile and Wenger's work (except repositories). I have pulled out examples which may need special note within Firmware Co-Design (standard examples such as diagrams, lists etc have been omitted)

  • Repositories (empty set)
  • Standardised forms and methods
    • Inventory [this could indicate the components of a device, mapping hardware/affordance]
    • Relations diagram [this could represent orchestrations of components]
    • Focus group interviews
  • Objects, models and maps
    • Prototypes [the disclosure and form of the device itself]
    • Mannequin [domain specific puppetry such as the paraglider]
    • Production games [intriguing and need to explore]

    The eight characteristics of boundary objects from the point of view of participants [ worth considering how many can be featured in the workshop]...

    • Objects-in-the-making- non ready-made, even if templated
    • Build-in-affordancesto make a design develop or articulate its quality, e.g. placing bricks in the layout game, socially forces you to explain why it's good.
    • Rules and instructions for usee.g. which color pen to use for marking up.
    • Facilitator-selected - the fact the experience is crafted by a facilitator was noted.
    • Temporary learning spacethe objects and context they create distance participants from daily routine and approaches.
    • Locationwhere objects are used is significant to the way people engage with them
    • Represent workshop outputsthe objects are a productive result of the effort and intent of the workshop, supporting incorporation into an ongoing design process, including a political character such as 'frozen boundary objects' presented to other actors.
