Participatory Design in a Human-Computer Interaction Course: Teaching Ethnography Methods to Computer Scientists

Authors

Notes

 They have explored using paper prototyping and ethnography within a HCI course, teaching computer scientists. I like their distinction between a Software Designer and a Software Engineer. Resonates very strongly with my own view.

Paper provides a nice overview of the intent and differences between techniques such as User-Centred Design, Human-Centered Systems, Participatory Design and Contextual Design. Particularly good summary of Ethnography.

Field of Study

Concepts

Contextual Design, Ethnography

Quotations

"Software designers need to apply methods that provide insights into the user's mental model of the application's target task and to invite the user to be an active participant in the design process."

"While the software programmer/engineer is concerned with developing reliable, robust, and maintainable software, the software designer is concerned with creating products that fit within the user's overall activities, enhance productivity, and produce a satisfying experience"

"Left to their own devices, computer programmers take a "systems-centered point of view", concerned about "how the software works and what parts of it do what" [8, p. 217-218]."

"Contextual design relies on ethnographic techniques from anthropology [10, 19, 15, 4, 5]. Ethnography is a method of research in which the researcher gathers data within a natural setting that involves a dynamic network o f interrelated variables. No attempt is made to control variables within the setting, for this could alter or destroy the phenomenon being studied. The purpose o f ethnographic research is to attempt to understand what is happening naturally in the setting, and through interpreting the data gathered to see what implications can be formed from the data."

"The final product produced from an ethnographic study should be a 'thick description" of the situation, so that the situation appears sufficiently realistic, and so that others in examining the description can determine whether the implications drawn from the study can be applied to other settings."

"HCI Students [after being taught Participatory Design] lamented that while they now understood the way design should be done, based on their experiences "unfortunately that is not the way it is done in the real world.""
