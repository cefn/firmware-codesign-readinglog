The myths of imagery

Authors

Terry Liddament

Notes

This is a philosophical piece which tries to nail down a taxonomy of imaging, including pictures, mental images, drawing and sketching.

"imaging and imagery...are often thought of as...mainsprings of insight and originality in design activity"

"What is in the picture-
frame is canvas, pigmented surface and so on; but what is in the picture,
that is, what it pictures, brings us to the threshold of a domain we cannot
enter unless we have grasped the concept of ‘picturing’, until we have
understood the function of a picture as a representation device 16 . If we
find this puzzling or surprising, then we should bear in mind that a picture
bears much more resemblance to the other ordinary material objects that
surround it than what it pictures. To be able to understand and use pictures
we must first acquire the requisite techniques of usage and this we do
partly by learning how to make pictures ourselves and also by attending
to the many uses of pictures in our culture. The central point here, is that
the capacity to see the content of pictures, what is ‘in’ them is a learned
capacity, acquired, like other usages of symbols (and language more
generally) through mastery of these techniques."

"to
see what is in the picture, it must become ‘transparent’ to us in a sense
which transcends the transparency of the primary conceptual field. To do
this one must grasp the use of the picture as a representational device, to
be able to see what is in the picture, that is, what it pictures. In grasping
this use of the picture, it becomes epistemically transparent and we have
learned a new skill which is (logically) quite distinct from that of the
‘informationally reliable’ reportage of the primary conceptual field."

"some pictures may be set up to offer a completely
unitary and unambiguous point-of-view; for example, an engineering draw-
ing or a set of explicit written instructions. When we view such a picture
it is intended (usually) that we only see it from one such possible point-
of-view. But in order for a picture to function, it is not vital that it has a
unitary epistemic stance as such. Indeed, many kinds of picture thrive on
ambiguities; they offer the possibility of a diversity of points-of-view. Thus
a viewer may contemplate the picture as representing different possibilities
(differing points-of-view) and different viewers may also ‘see’ different
things in the picture."

"‘ambiguity’, far from being an accidental by-product of the techniques of picturing, has the potential to lead into
entirely new domains; through this enhanced use of pictures we acquire
the power to express what-might-be."

"if the discourse we employ with respect to concepts
such as imaging, imagery and other mental, psychological states could only
ever be applied to ‘inner’ essentially private phenomena, then it would be
impossible to make sense of it. If our ordinary everyday language is to
have any use for us, it must refer to a domain we can all share."
